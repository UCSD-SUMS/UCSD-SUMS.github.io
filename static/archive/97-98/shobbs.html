Stephen Hobbs

Major/Degree: Ph.D, Mathematics, 1985

Why did you choose math as a major? I enjoyed working on math problems, not so much games and puzzles with (what I perceived as) no applications but on problems that might relate to science and technology.

Favorite Math Professor: Stephan Warschowski. This is a hard question because there are so many excellent prof's at UCSD, but Warschowski was so meticulous and clear in lectures that he contributed a lot to my professional growth as a young grad student and left a lasting impression on me.

Favorite Mathematician: Lars Hormander. This is also a hard question because there are so many really significant names in any branch of mathematics (PDE in this case) and anyone's learning necessarily owes to quite a few of them. But I fell in love with PDE after looking at Hormander's '63 book as a grad student, well before I could really understand it; he is still the single most inspiring author for me.

Employer Name: Space and Naval Warfare (SPAWAR) Systems Center (SSC)

Employer Description: SSC is a Navy lab that develops sonar, radar, communications, and command and control systems for the Navy.

Job Title: Scientist / Mathematician

Job Description: Provide mathematical support for engineering projects and engineers at SSC.

Skills used:

Mathematical: Calculus and Linear Algebra, 'Pure' Analysis (real, complex, manifolds, functional), 'Applied' Analysis (differential and integral equations - we use the Helmholtz equation for wave propagation a lot - calculus of variations, integral transforms and series expansions, special functions, etc), Probability (lots of stochastic processes and harmonic analysis of time series, occasionally Ito's ODEs and integrals, stochastic filtering and control), Statistics (LOTS OF THIS, regression, analysis of variance, multivariate analysis, discrimination and classification, reliability tests, maximum likelihood estimation, time series analysis), Numerical Methods (LOTS OF THIS, linear algebra, ODEs and PDEs, optimization), Engineering and Physics (signal processing, acoustics, electromagnetic wave propagation, continuum mechanics).

Computer: Non-technical Applications Software (email, word processing, spread sheets, graphics and presentation vugraphs -MS Office and Eudora seem to be our standards here), Technical Applications Software (mathematical packages -Matlab is almost ubiquitous here-, statistics packages -I like S+), Programming Languages (C -I can't tolerate it but it's what most people use-, Fortran is still in use).

Interpersonal: This is important, especially if you want to move up in management. Many of our technical people have rather poor interpersonal skills in the sense of being graceful, and if you want to stay in technical work that's not much of a disadvantage. But truthfulness is important for a good technical person; managers will respect a technical person who tells them the truth about a project (eg, that it won't work), and not just what someone higher up wants to hear (eg, that it will work). It is also important for technical people to be able to present results, both formally and informally, clearly and as simply as possible; some technical people are very smart but they give complicated answers to simple questions, and their colleagues are soon worn out by the mental load of trying to figure out what they just said.

How did you come to choose a career in this field? In school I developed a love for mathematical analysis; I wanted a job that gave me a chance to use it. I also felt that school (at least the courses I took) left me pretty ignorant of applications of mathematics, so I talked to someone working as an applied mathematician at the Navy lab and found out that they used quite a bit of mathematics in their work, far more than just undergraduate or even beginning graduate level stuff. I felt that a few years in a job like that would serve me as a 'post doc' in applied math and enhance both my math skills and my knowledge of applications.

What you like most about your job? The chance to use mathematics, some of it fairly advanced, to help people (engineers in my case) solve applied quantitative problems. I should also say that much of this 'problem solving' really amounts to helping them understand their problems using (mathematical) tools that are more general than what they've seen in their training.

What you like least about your job? I sometimes miss teaching math courses. If I were in academics I would perhaps miss being involved with significant applications; being in applications I miss the chance to develop a set of lecture notes on the basics of a topic and explain it to students.

Advice for undergrads: Give attention to your 'core' courses; learn to compute things, but also learn to do proofs because clear, logical thinking is one of the most important skills you can bring to your job.
