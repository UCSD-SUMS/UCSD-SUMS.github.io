Richard Libby
Richard A. Libby (Revelle, 1980, B.A., Mathematics) continued work in pure mathematics, obtaining an M.A. from UCSD in 1982 and a Ph.D. from UC Santa Cruz in 1990. He chose math as a major after having enjoyed the intellectual appeal of analytical methods behind the calculus learned in high school. Further research led to a doctoral dissertation in Functional Analysis. Finding more opportunities in applications than in academic research, Richard entered the finance profession and now works in Risk Management for Bank of America, designing systems to support the Bank's efforts to measure market and credit exposure for its global trading activities. In his current position as Vice President and Senior Manager of Technical Finance, Richard manages three quantitative analysts who measure market driven credit exposures and develop pricing methods for new derivatives transactions. He recently completed a project as principal architect of the bank's global market risk system.

In spite of his non-academic career path, Richard continues to follow the latest advances in mathematics, by maintaining membership in the A.M.S. and S.I.A.M., reading up on a recent article on his favorite living mathematician, Michael Freedman, who has augmented his position with UCSD faculty by joining the research team at Microsoft, where he carries out research on the P=NP problem. Richard attended Prof. Freedman's seminars just after the announcement of the solution of the Poincare conjecture in four dimensions, and remembers the analysis of "hairy red blood cells" and "six-fold group death."

If the study of mathematics is irresistible, then by all means study mathematics. The time spent in the university is a substantial fraction of the time allotted on earth and should be considered, in part, an ends in itself. Mathematics, even pure mathematics, is not a field of study restricted to future university professors.
